Chaos Engineering: the history, principles, and practice

Chaos Engineering is a disciplined approach to identifying failures before they become outages. By proactively testing how a system responds under stress, you can identify and fix failures before they end up in the news. That helps you to build more resilient systems

What are the Principles of Chaos Engineering?

Why would you break things on purpose?

Chaos Engineering is a tool we use to build such an immunity in our technical systems by injecting harm (like latency, CPU failure, or network black holes) in order to find and mitigate potential weaknesses.

By breaking things on purpose we surface unknown issues that could impact our systems and customers.

Fallacies of Distributed Systems:

  • The network is reliable

  • Latency is zero

  • Bandwidth is infinite

  • The network is secure

  • Topology doesn't change

  • There is one administrator

  • Transport cost is zero

  • The network is homogeneous

What are the customer, business, and technical benefits of Chaos Engineering?

  • Customer: No outages disrupt their day-to-day lives.

  • Business:

    • Prevent extremely large losses in revenue and maintenance costs

    • Create happier and more engaged engineers

    • Improve in on-call training for engineering teams

    • Improve the incident management program

  • Technical:

    • Reduction in incidents

    • Reduction in on-call burden

    • Increased understanding of system failure modes

    • Improved system design

    • Faster mean time to detection for SEVs, and reduction in repeated SEVs.

Which Chaos Engineering experiments do you perform first?

Order

  1. Known knowns

  2. Known unknowns

  3. Unknown knowns

  4. Unknown unknowns

Planning your First Experiment

Which scenarios are more likely (or more frightening) and should be tested first.

Creating a Hypothesis

What will be the impact to customers, to your service, or to your dependencies?

Measuring the Impact

It is good to have a key performance metric that correlates to customer success (such as orders per minute).

As a rule of thumb, if you ever see an impact to these metrics, you want to halt the experiment immediately.

Next is measuring the failure itself where you want to verify (or disprove) your hypothesis.

  • This could be the impact on latency, requests per second, or system resources.

  • Lastly, you want to survey your dashboards and alarms for unintended side effects.

Have a Rollback Plan

All of our attack's must can be reverted immediately, allowing you to safely abort and return to steady state if things go wrong.
